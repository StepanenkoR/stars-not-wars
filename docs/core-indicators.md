# Core Indicators

This document defines the first core indicators of Stars Not Wars.

These indicators are not meant to describe all of civilization.  
They are the first small set of signals that help us see a central question of this project:

**How does humanity allocate its resources — toward destruction or toward life?**

Version 0.1 should stay minimal.  
The purpose is not to track everything, but to identify a small number of indicators that are meaningful, measurable, and useful for future comparison.

---

## Principles for selecting indicators

The first indicators should be:

- globally relevant
- measurable across countries or across time
- available from public and relatively stable sources
- useful for civilizational comparison
- simple enough to build first artifacts around them

These indicators are not final.  
They are the first working backbone of the project.

---

## Core Indicator 1 — Military Expenditure

### Why it matters
Military expenditure is one of the clearest measurable signals of how much civilization is willing to spend on organized destruction, coercion, and preparation for war.

It does not describe all violence, but it gives a strong baseline for the scale of militarized resource allocation.

### What it helps reveal
- how much is spent on war-oriented systems
- how military spending changes over time
- which countries allocate the most to militarization
- how large the war system is compared to other sectors

### Possible forms
- military expenditure (current USD)
- military expenditure as % of GDP
- military expenditure per capita

---

## Core Indicator 2 — GDP

### Why it matters
GDP is not a moral indicator by itself, but it provides a basic measure of economic scale.

Without GDP, it is difficult to understand whether military spending or other resource allocations are large relative to the productive capacity of a country or of humanity as a whole.

### What it helps reveal
- the scale of total economic output
- relative burden of military expenditure
- capacity for redirection of resources
- baseline context for all other indicators

### Possible forms
- GDP (current USD)
- GDP per capita
- global GDP

---

## Core Indicator 3 — Energy Use

### Why it matters
Energy is one of the deepest physical foundations of civilization.

If war shows how much we spend on destruction, energy shows part of the material capacity of civilization to build, sustain complexity, and move toward abundance.

Energy is also important because any long-term civilization pathway — including planetary maturity, radical technological growth, or future stellar-scale systems — depends on large-scale energy access and use.

### What it helps reveal
- the physical scale of civilization
- differences in material development
- capacity for infrastructure, industry, and future growth
- whether civilization is developing the foundations for long-term expansion

### Possible forms
- energy use per capita
- total energy consumption
- electricity generation
- energy production by source

---

## Core Indicator 4 — Life Expectancy

### Why it matters
Life expectancy is one of the simplest and strongest indicators of whether civilization is actually serving life.

A civilization can be rich, militarized, and technologically advanced while still failing to produce long, healthy human lives.

Life expectancy is not enough by itself, but it is a necessary counterweight to purely economic or military measures.

### What it helps reveal
- the real human outcome of civilization
- whether material development translates into lived benefit
- whether societies are preserving life or wasting it
- long-term direction of human well-being

### Possible forms
- life expectancy at birth
- healthy life expectancy (later, if available)

---

## Core Indicator 5 — Research and Development (R&D)

### Why it matters
R&D is one of the clearest measurable signals that a civilization is investing in future capability rather than only in present consumption or destruction.

If military expenditure represents organized preparation for conflict, R&D can represent organized preparation for knowledge, innovation, medicine, energy systems, and long-horizon development.

### What it helps reveal
- whether civilization is investing in its future
- how much capacity is being built for discovery and problem-solving
- comparison between war-oriented and knowledge-oriented allocation
- potential foundations for scientific and technological transition

### Possible forms
- R&D expenditure (% of GDP)
- total R&D expenditure
- public vs private R&D (later, if available)

---

## Why these five

These five indicators form a simple first structure:

- **Military Expenditure** → destruction
- **GDP** → total economic scale
- **Energy Use** → physical capacity
- **Life Expectancy** → real human outcome
- **R&D** → future-oriented capability

Together, they do not explain everything.  
But together, they already begin to describe whether civilization is spending itself on war, or moving toward life, growth, and long-term development.

## What comes next

After defining the core indicators, the next step is not to collect everything at once.

The next step is to:

1. identify one reliable source for each indicator
2. define a minimal JSON structure
3. create the first small data artifacts
4. compare the first indicators in simple research notes

---

## Version 0.1 priority

Version 0.1 should focus on these five indicators only.

More can be added later:
- health expenditure
- education
- scientific output
- poverty
- infrastructure
- emissions
- conflict losses
- longevity-related indicators

But first, the project needs a stable backbone.

These five indicators are that backbone.
